Recall that the first fundamental form \(       \mathrm{I} \colon T_pM \times T_pM \to \mathbb{R}     \) is defined intrinsically as the map \[       \mathrm{I}(a \mathbf{r}_u(p) + b \mathbf{r}_v(p), c \mathbf{r}_u(p) + d \mathbf{r}_v(p)) = acE + (ad+bc)F + bdG,     \] where \[       \begin{align*}         E = \langle \mathbf{r}_u, \mathbf{r}_u \rangle, \quad         F = \langle \mathbf{r}_u, \mathbf{r}_v \rangle, \quad         G = \langle \mathbf{r}_v, \mathbf{r}_v \rangle.       \end{align*}     \]
We typically write this as \[       \mathrm{I} = E du^2 + 2F du\, dv + G dv^2     \] or in the matrix notation (with respect to the basis \(\{ \mathbf{r}_u, \mathbf{r}_v \} \)) \[       \mathrm{I}(\mathbf{w}_1,\mathbf{w}_2) = \langle \mathbf{w}_1,\mathbf{w}_2 \rangle = \mathbf{w}_1^T \begin{pmatrix}       E & F \\ F & G \end{pmatrix} \mathbf{w}_2.     \]


Now the second fundamental form \[       \mathrm{I\!I} \colon T_pM \times T_pM \to \mathbb{R}     \] is defined extrinsically with respect to the Gauss map \(       \mathbf{N} \colon M \to S^2     \) and its differential \( d\mathbf{N}_p \colon T_pM \to T_{\mathbf{N}(p)}(S^2) \), namely: \[       \mathrm{I\!I}(\mathbf{w}_1,\mathbf{w}_2) = \langle -d\mathbf{N}_p(\mathbf{w}_1), \mathbf{w}_2 \rangle = \langle P(\mathbf{w}_1), \mathbf{w}_2 \rangle.     \]
Here, \( P = -d\mathbf{N}_p \) is called the shape operator.1


We will prove that with respect to the basis \(\{ \mathbf{r}_u, \mathbf{r}_v \} \), \[       \mathrm{I\!I}(\mathbf{w}_1,\mathbf{w}_2) = \mathbf{w}_1^T \begin{pmatrix} L & M \\ M & N\end{pmatrix} \mathbf{w}_2,     \] where \[       L = -\mathbf{r}_{uu} \cdot \mathbf{N}, \quad       M = -\mathbf{r}_{uv} \cdot \mathbf{N}, \quad       N = -\mathbf{r}_{vv} \cdot \mathbf{N}.     \]
(Here, \( M \) is a real number, and is different from our surface \( M \).)


Lastly, we will use the first and second fundamental forms (which are easy to compute) to determine the Gaussian and mean curvatures at every point on our surface.

  \item The partial derivatives of the inner product
        with respect to \(u\) is zero, so we have
        \[
          \frac{\partial}{\partial u} \langle \mathbf{r}_u, \mathbf{N} \rangle = \langle \mathbf{r}_{uu}, \mathbf{N} \rangle + \langle \mathbf{r}_u, \mathbf{N}_u \rangle = 0.
        \]
        Since the shape operator is \(P = -d\mathbf{N}_p \), we have
        \[
          \langle \mathbf{r}_{uu}, \mathbf{N} \rangle + \langle \mathbf{r}_u, \mathbf{N}_u \rangle = \langle \mathbf{r}_{uu}, \mathbf{N} \rangle + \langle \mathbf{r}_u, P(\mathbf{r}_u) \rangle = 0.
        \]
        Therefore, we obtain \( \langle \mathbf{N},  \mathbf{r}_{uu} \rangle = \langle P(\mathbf{r}_{u}), \mathbf{r}_{u} \rangle \).

  \item %Extend this idea to prove that with respect to the basis \(\{ \mathbf{r}_u, \mathbf{r}_v \} \), \[         \langle P(\mathbf{w}_1), \mathbf{w}_2 \rangle = \mathbf{w}_1^T \begin{pmatrix} L & M \\ M & N\end{pmatrix} \mathbf{w}_2.       \] Now we want to recover the eigenvalues of the shape operator \( P \). Recall that our inner product is precisely \( \mathrm{I} \), and thus \[         \langle P(\mathbf{w}_1), \mathbf{w}_2 \rangle = P(\mathbf{w}_1)^T \begin{pmatrix} E & F \\ F & G \end{pmatrix} \mathbf{w}_2.       \] Use this to prove that \[         P(\mathbf{w}_1) = \begin{pmatrix} E & F \\ F & G \end{pmatrix}^{-1}\begin{pmatrix} L & M \\ M & N\end{pmatrix} \mathbf{w}_1.       \]
        Let \(L = \langle \mathbf{N}, \mathbf{r}_{uu} \rangle\), \(M = \langle \mathbf{N}, \mathbf{r}_{uv} \rangle\), and \(N = \langle \mathbf{N}, \mathbf{r}_{vv} \rangle\)
        also let \(w_1=a\mathbf{r}_u + b\mathbf{r}_v\) and \(w_2=c\mathbf{r}_u + d\mathbf{r}_v\). Then we have
        therefore,
        \[
          \langle P(\mathbf{w}_1), \mathbf{w}_2 \rangle = \langle P(a\mathbf{r}_u + b\mathbf{r}_v), c\mathbf{r}_u + d\mathbf{r}_v \rangle = acL + (ad+bc)M + bdN.
        \]
        Since as matrixs, \(\mathbf{w}_1 = (a,b)\) and \(\mathbf{w}_2 = (c,d)\), we have
        \[
          \langle P(\mathbf{w}_1), \mathbf{w}_2 \rangle = \mathbf{w}_1^T \begin{pmatrix} L & M \\ M & N\end{pmatrix} \mathbf{w}_2.
        \]
  \item Since \(I = \begin{pmatrix}
          E & F \\ F & G
        \end{pmatrix}\) and we know that \(
        \langle P(\mathbf{w}_1), \mathbf{w}_2 \rangle = P(\mathbf{w}_1)^T I \mathbf{w}_2\),  and earlier
        we showed that \(\langle P(\mathbf{w}_1), \mathbf{w}_2 \rangle = \mathbf{w}_1^T \begin{pmatrix} L & M \\ M & N\end{pmatrix} \mathbf{w}_2\), we have
        \[
          P(\mathbf{w}_1)^T I \mathbf{w}_2 = \mathbf{w}_1^T \begin{pmatrix} L & M \\ M & N\end{pmatrix} \mathbf{w}_2.
        \]
        We can divide by \(\mathbf{w}_2\) on both sides and then transpose to get
        \[
          I P(\mathbf{w}_1) = \begin{pmatrix} L & M \\ M & N\end{pmatrix} \mathbf{w}_1.
        \]
        Since \(I\) is invertible, we have that
        \[
          P(\mathbf{w}_1) = I^{-1} \begin{pmatrix} L & M \\ M & N\end{pmatrix} \mathbf{w}_1.
        \]

  \item By definition, the Gaussian curvature \(K\) is the determinant of the shape operator \(P\), We can use the fact that before we showed how \(P = I^{-1}\begin{pmatrix} L & M \\ M & N\end{pmatrix}\) so that
        \[
          K = \det(P) = \det\left(I^{-1}\begin{pmatrix} L & M \\ M & N\end{pmatrix}\right) = \det(I^{-1})\det\left(\begin{pmatrix} L & M \\ M & N\end{pmatrix}\right).
        \]
        The determinant of \(I^{-1}\) is the reciprocal of the determinant of \(I\), and \(\det(I) = EG-F^2\), and the determinant of \(\begin{pmatrix} L & M \\ M & N\end{pmatrix}\) is \(LN-M^2\), so we have that
        \[
          K = \frac{LN-M^2}{EG-F^2}.
        \]

  \item By definition, the mean curvature \(H\) is half the trace of the shape operator \(P\), so we have
        \(
        H = \frac{1}{2} \operatorname{tr}(P).
        \)
        The function \(P\) written out all the way is
        \[
          P = \frac{1}{EG-F^2}\begin{pmatrix} G & -F \\ -F & E \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} L & M \\ M & N\end{pmatrix}.
        \]
        The trace of \(P\) is
        \[
          \operatorname{tr}(P) = \frac{1}{EG-F^2} (GL - 2FM + EN).
        \]
        Therefore,
        \[
          H = \frac{GL - 2FM + EN}{2(EG-F^2)}.
        \]

  \item Given the function \[         \mathbf{r}(u,v) = \begin{pmatrix} u \\ v \\ uv\end{pmatrix},\quad (u,v) \in (-1,1) \times (-1, 1),       \]
        at the point \(p = \mathbf{r}(0,0)\) has the following partial derivatives:
        \[
          \mathbf{r}_u = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix},\quad
          \mathbf{r}_v = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix},\quad
          \mathbf{r}_{uu} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix},\quad
          \mathbf{r}_{uv} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix},\quad
          \mathbf{r}_{vv} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}.
        \]
        The first fundamental coefficients are
        \[
          E = \langle \mathbf{r}_u, \mathbf{r}_u \rangle = 1, \quad
          F = \langle \mathbf{r}_u, \mathbf{r}_v \rangle = 0, \quad
          G = \langle \mathbf{r}_v, \mathbf{r}_v \rangle = 1.
        \]
        The unit normal vector is
        \[
        \mathbf{N} = \frac{\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v}{\|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v\|} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}.
        \]
        The second fundamental coefficients are
        \[
          L = \langle \mathbf{N}, \mathbf{r}_{uu} \rangle = 0, \quad
          M = \langle \mathbf{N}, \mathbf{r}_{uv} \rangle = 1, \quad
          N = \langle \mathbf{N}, \mathbf{r}_{vv} \rangle = 0.
        \]
        Therefore, the first and second fundamental forms are
        \[
          \mathrm{I} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad
          \mathrm{I\!I} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}.
        \]
        The shape operator is \(P = \mathrm{I}^{-1}\mathrm{I\!I}=\begin{pmatrix}
          0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0
        \end{pmatrix}\) which we can get the eigenvalues of by solving the characteristic polynomial \(\lambda^2 - 1 = 0\) to get \(\lambda = \pm 1\). Therefore, the Gaussian curvature is \(K = \kappa_1\kappa_2 = -1\) and the mean curvature is \(H = \frac{1}{2} (\kappa_1 + \kappa_2) = 0\).
